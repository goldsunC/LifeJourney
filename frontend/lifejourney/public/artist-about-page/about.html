Biography

The Journey of an
Visionary Artist

Elena Mori (b. 1985, Milan, Italy) is a contemporary visual artist whose work transcends traditional boundaries between mediums and disciplines. Her artistic practice spans painting, sculpture, digital media, and large-scale installations, all unified by a profound exploration of perception, memory, and the fluid nature of reality.

Born into a family of architects and musicians, Mori was exposed to diverse creative influences from an early age. This multicultural background—her mother is Japanese-Italian, her father Spanish-French—instilled in her a unique perspective on cultural hybridity that would later become a central theme in her work.

Mori's formal artistic training began at the Accademia di Belle Arti di Brera in Milan, where she studied traditional painting techniques before pursuing further education at the Royal College of Art in London, where she explored experimental media and installation art.

"Art is not about reproducing what we see, but revealing what we cannot see—those hidden connections between the physical world and our internal landscapes."
— Elena Mori

After graduating with distinction in 2009, Mori established her first studio in Berlin, a city whose vibrant art scene and historical complexity profoundly influenced her early development. It was during this period that she began merging traditional oil painting with digital projections, creating immersive environments that challenge viewers' perceptions of space and form.

Today, based between New York and Tokyo, Mori continues to push artistic boundaries, incorporating emerging technologies such as augmented reality and interactive elements into her practice while maintaining a deep connection to classical artistic principles.

Artistic Vision

Philosophy & Creative Process

The Nature of Perception

At the core of Mori's practice is an investigation into how our perceptions shape—and often distort—our understanding of reality. Her work consistently challenges viewers to question their assumptions about what is "real," exploring the space between objective experience and subjective interpretation.

"Reality is not fixed but fluid," Mori explains. "It exists in the tension between external stimuli and our internal filters—memories, cultural conditioning, emotional states. My art seeks to make that tension visible."

Creative Methodology

Mori's creative process is deeply research-driven, often beginning with extensive studies in neuroscience, psychology, and philosophy. She meticulously documents her findings in notebooks that serve as the conceptual foundation for her work.

From these theoretical explorations, she develops physical sketches and digital models, iterating extensively before settling on a final direction. This rigorous approach ensures that each piece carries intellectual weight while maintaining strong visual impact.

Materiality plays a crucial role in her practice. Mori often works with unconventional materials—industrial resins, conductive inks, light-reactive compounds—alongside traditional ones like oil paint and bronze. This juxtaposition of old and new reflects her interest in the dialogue between historical artistic traditions and contemporary technological advancements.

Evolution of Style

While Mori's early work focused primarily on painting, her style has evolved dramatically over the years. Her Berlin period (2009-2014) saw the introduction of digital elements and interactive components. Her move to New York in 2015 marked a shift toward larger-scale installations and site-specific works that engage with architectural space.

More recently, her Tokyo studio has influenced a return to more minimalist aesthetics, with an emphasis on negative space and subtle tonal variations—a reflection of traditional Japanese artistic principles merged with her Western training.
